Abdominal Infection Guidelines

Antimicrobial dosing is based on a patient with normal renal function. For dosing based on impaired renal function, see Dosing of Antimicrobial Agents.

An appropriate source control procedure to drain infected foci, control ongoing peritoneal contamination by diversion or resection, and restore anatomic and physiological function to the extent feasible is recommended for nearly all patients with intra-abdominal infection.

The following recommendations are empiric guidelines. Base definitive choice of antimicrobial agents on operative cultures and susceptibility test results.

These guidelines may not be appropriate in patients with history of colonization or infection with resistant organisms or recent antimicrobial exposure. For these patients, consider Infectious Diseases consultation.

Antimicrobial therapy of established intra-abdominal infection should be limited to 5 days in most patients, unless it is difficult to achieve adequate source control

  • When signs and symptoms of infection are resolved, no further antibiotic therapy is typically required
  • In patients who have persistent or recurrent clinical evidence of intra-abdominal infection after 5 days of therapy, appropriate diagnostic investigation should be undertaken (usually cross-sectional imaging with CT or MRI)
  • Extra-abdominal sources of infection and non-infectious inflammatory conditions should also be investigated if the patient is not experiencing a satisfactory clinical response to an appropriate empiric antimicrobial regimen initial empiric antimicrobial regimen

Patients who have undergone cholecystectomy for acute cholecystitis should have antimicrobial therapy discontinued within 24 hours unless there is evidence of infection outside the wall of the gallbladder

Bowel injuries attributable to penetrating, blunt, or iatrogenic trauma that are repaired within 12 hours and any other intraoperative contamination of the operative field by enteric contents should be treated with antibiotics for â‰¤ 24 hours

Acute appendicitis without evidence of perforation, abscess, or local peritonitis requires only prophylactic administration of narrow spectrum regimens active against aerobic and facultative and obligate anaerobes (e.g. ceftriaxone 1 gram IV daily and metronidazole 500 mg PO or IV Q12H). Treatment should be discontinued within 24 hours.

If culture and susceptibility testing identify organisms that are only susceptible to intravenous therapy, this therapy should be continued as an outpatient to finish the course. For patients recovering from intra-abdominal infection, completion of a course with oral antibiotics is acceptable in patients able to tolerate an oral diet.
