Candida Empiric Treatment

Overview

Infections caused by Candida species are a major source of morbidity and mortality, and can cause a wide spectrum of clinical disease.

Several Candida species carry unique characteristics that become clinically relevant in terms of treatment of these infections. The following table summarizes general susceptibility patterns across Candida species with commonly used antifungal agents. These qualitative susceptibility descriptions are meant to provide a conceptual framework of effective drugs so should not be used as quantitative data for susceptibility of Candida species locally.

Local susceptibilities of common Candida species

1C. glabrata isolates that are susceptible-dose dependent to fluconazole require higher dosing to achieve optimal activity

2C. krusei is inherently resistant to fluconazole and variably sensitive to voriconazole and should be treated with an echinocandin unless voriconazole susceptibility is confirmed.

3C. auris is an emerging multidrug-resistant species and should be treated with an echinocandin empirically.

Invasive candidiasis encompasses candidemia, defined as the presence of Candida species in the blood, and deep organ infections caused by Candida species.

Infectious Diseases consultation is strongly recommended for any patient with invasive candidiasis. Initial management should include removal of all existing central venous catheters. All patients with candidemia should undergo at least one ophthalmological examination to exclude the possibility of candidal endophthalmitis or retinitis.

The table below includes recommendations for empiric treatment of presumed invasive candidiasis. Base definitive choice of antifungal agents on blood or tissue cultures and antifungal susceptibility results. Transition to step-down therapy with fluconazole is recommended in clinically stable patients with fluconazole-susceptible isolates and negative surveillance blood cultures.

Transition from intravenous to oral fluconazole is recommended in patients who can tolerate oral medications due to high bioavailability.

Empiric Therapy Duration/Comments
Candidemia

Preferred: Caspofungin 70 mg IV load x 1 dose, followed by 50 mg IV daily

Alternatives: Fluconazole 800 mg (12mg/kg) IV load, followed by 400 mg (6mg/kg) IV/PO daily OR Liposomal Amphotericin B 3-5 mg/kg IV daily

Uncomplicated candidemia should be treated for 14 days after first negative blood culture + resolution of signs and symptoms.

Durations of 4-6 weeks should be used if blood cultures persistently positive, metastatic foci identified or undrained abscesses present.
Endocarditis

Preferred: Liposomal Amphotericin B 3-5 mg/kg IV daily +/- Flucytosine 25mg/kg PO four times daily

Alternatives: Caspofungin 150mg IV daily

Durations of 6 weeks should be used under the direction of an Infectious Diseases consultation. Cardiothoracic surgery consultation should be considered if a patient is a candidate for valve replacement.
CNS Infections

Preferred: Liposomal Amphotericin B 3-5 mg/kg IV daily +/- Flucytosine 25mg/kg PO four times daily

Alternatives: Fluconazole 800 mg (12mg/kg) IV load, followed by 400 mg (6mg/kg) IV/PO daily

Use of an echinocandin should be avoided due to poor CNS penetration.

Durations of 4-6 weeks should be used under the direction of an Infectious Diseases consultation.
Ocular Infections

Preferred: Fluconazole 800 mg (12mg/kg) IV load, followed by 400 mg (6mg/kg) IV/PO daily

Alternatives: Liposomal Amphotericin B 3-5 mg/kg IV daily +/- Flucytosine 25mg/kg PO four times daily

Use of an echinocandin should be avoided due to poor ocular tissue penetration.

Durations of 4-6 weeks should be used under the direction of an Infectious Diseases and ophthalmology consultation.
Osteoarticular Infections

Preferred: Caspofungin 70 mg IV load x 1 dose, followed by 50 mg IV daily

Alternative: Fluconazole 800 mg (12mg/kg) IV load, followed by 400 mg (6mg/kg) IV/PO daily

Durations of 4-6 weeks should be used for the treatment of septic arthritis under the direction of an Infectious Diseases consultation.

Durations of 6-12 months should be used for the treatment of osteomyelitis under the direction of an Infectious Diseases consultation.

Candiduria is the presence of Candida species in the urine.

Asymptomatic candiduria should NOT routinely be treated, but could be considered in neutropenic patients or when patients undergo invasive urologic procedures with anticipated risk of bleeding.

In cases of symptomatic candiduria, management should include removal of indwelling urinary catheters and hardware in the genitourinary tract. Failure to do so will result in higher rates of treatment failure.

In cases of treatment failure, identification and susceptibility testing should be performed on the isolate.

Use of an echinocandin should be avoided due to poor urinary concentrations.

Symptomatic Cystitis

Vulvovaginal
