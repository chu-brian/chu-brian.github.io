Bone and Joint Infections

Overview

Antimicrobial dosing is based on a patient with normal renal function. For dosing based on impaired renal function, see Dosing of Antimicrobial Agents.

Includes infections that are secondary to hematogenous spread, those arising from a contiguous focus, including post-traumatic and post-operative infections, and those complicated by the presence of foreign material or implanted hardware.

For ideal treatment:

  • Infections should be treated by debridement of infected and necrotic bone.
  • All dead-space should be eliminated by the placement of bone grafts or muscle flaps.
  • Infections require soft-tissue coverage to promote revascularization.
  • Infections require prolonged antibiotics courses directed against the organisms isolated from culture of the bone.

Empiric antibiotic therapy is often NOT required if surgery is planned unless patient has systemic signs of infection (fever, severe sepsis, etc.). Instead, antibiotics should be deferred until after surgery and based on culture results. It is highly desirable to base antibiotic choices on bone or tissue cultures rather than wound or sinus swabs. Obtain cultures from blood (≥2 sets) plus bone and deep tissue samples (≥3 sets, ideally 5 or 6) at the time of debridement to guide antibiotic treatment.

The route of antibiotic administration can be parenteral (IV) or oral, provided the oral bioavailability of individual agents is high.

Infected hardware complicates management of osteomyelitis. Debridement without hardware removal is associated with lower cure rates. However, fracture instability and non-union are a consequence of inadequate fixation, and the application of external fixation can be challenging or not at all possible.

When possible infected hardware should be removed in staged approach (hardware removal, interval antibiotic treatment followed by fixation with new hardware or bone grafting).

In most cases, infection arises secondary to hematogenous spread to the joint, although it also can arise from a bite or from other direct traumatic inoculation and rarely, from direct inoculation of bacteria during joint procedures.

Treatment requires adequate drainage of purulent joint fluid and appropriate antimicrobial therapy.

Obtain blood, joint fluid, and other appropriate cultures (e.g. throat, vaginal, urethral, rectal if Neisseria gonorrhoeae is suspected) before starting empiric antimicrobial therapy.

Prosthetic joint infections (PJI) may occur in 1% of patients undergoing total hip and total knee arthroplasty, and less commonly in patients undergoing shoulder arthroplasty. Once a patient has had a PJI, their risk of subsequent PJI rises.

Early-onset PJI (infection within 90 days of implantation) is often caused by peri-operative contamination or seeding.

Late-onset PJI may have several causes including non-healing wound, joint trauma, or intercurrent bacteremia with hematogenous dissemination and seeding of the joint.

Optimal management of PJI is a two-step exchange as it provides the best chance for cure of infection. However, in some subpopulations of patients a two-step exchange is not feasible.

The decision to use oral antibiotic therapy for a short period after joint re-implantation, or for chronic suppression is not a standard recommendation, should be considered on a case-by-csae basis, and should only be considered in the setting of Infectious Diseases consultation.
